Bouldering in Fontainebleau

Created2025-05-03|Adventure
|Word count:494|Reading time:3min|Post View:

2025.4.18 - 2025.4.19
Fontainebleau, France

Over the Easter holidays 2025, I escaped the city with my climbing friends and headed to the legendary Fontainebleau forest for two days of outdoor bouldering. With enjoyable sunshine, cool air, and a car full of crash pads, we explored the sandstone blocs that have shaped generations of climbers. We rented a car and stayed in the Fontainebleau town and we rent four pads locally.

Day 1: Bas Cuvier

We kicked things off at Bas Cuvier, one of the most iconic and accessible bouldering areas in Bleau. The forest was alive with climbers, birdsong, and filtered sunlight through the trees. Conditions were ideal — dry rock and just cool enough for good friction.

We warmed up on a series of fun grade 5 problems, each with its own style and quirks. Fontainebleau has a way of making even the “easy” stuff technical and engaging.

The forest didn’t waste time humbling me — on my very first problem, I had a bit of an accident while topping out. In a moment of poor body control, I smacked my face on the rock and chipped a tiny piece off one of my front teeth. Nothing serious, but enough to make me be careful about top-out. From that point on, I kept my mouth shut every time I mantled.

A standout moment was trying La Marie Rose (6A) — famously the first 6A in the world. I didn’t send it, but just standing at the base of that piece of climbing history was surreal.

Day 2: Rocher Canon

On the second day, we headed to Rocher Canon, which felt more secluded and peaceful compared to Cuvier. The problems here are spread out among beautiful pine-covered trails, and we had long stretches of time where it felt like we had the whole forest to ourselves.

The highlight of the day — and probably the whole trip — was sending Sixième Piste (6B+). It’s a cavr problem with a committing crux. With plenty of pads under me and friends spotting, I finally linked the moves and topped out. There’s another problem which is both fun and beautiful, with the first half overhangging and second half slabbing. After topping out, I saw a “caterpillar train” — around tens of caterpillars connected to each other with heads and tails, which was so creepy.

Summary

Fontainebleau is more than just a climbing destination — it’s a place with deep history, unique style, and a rhythm all its own. Every problem feels like a riddle, and every fall teaches you something.

One thing that really stood out to me this trip was how sandbagged the problem is. A 5 in Bleau can easily feel like a 6A elsewhere, especially if you’re not used to the polished footholds, delicate slabs, or slopey mantles. Grades here are humble — and sometimes humbling — but in the best possible way.

I would not reject the chance to pay a second visit here:)
